## The Origins of the Samurai Class
The samurai class emerged in the 12th century, with the first recorded samurai being Minamoto no Yoshitsune in 1184. These warriors were known for their **bravery**, **loyalty**, and adherence to the **Bushido code**, which emphasized **courage**, **veracity**, and **self-discipline**. Samurai were also expected to be well-versed in **literature** and **poetry**, making them a unique blend of warrior and scholar. By the 16th century, there were over 500,000 samurai in Japan, making them a dominant force in Japanese society. Some key aspects of the samurai class include:
* **Martial arts training**: Samurai were trained in various forms of martial arts, including kenjutsu, jujutsu, and kyujutsu.
* **Literary and poetic pursuits**: Samurai were expected to be well-versed in literature and poetry, with many famous samurai being renowned for their literary skills.
* **Adherence to the Bushido code**: Samurai were expected to live by the principles of the Bushido code, which emphasized courage, veracity, and self-discipline.

## The Daily Life of a Samurai
A samurai's daily life was one of discipline and dedication. **Samurai training** began at a young age, typically around 5 or 6 years old. They would learn **martial arts**, **literature**, and **philosophy**, with the most famous samurai school being the Yagyu Ryu, founded by Yagyu Munenori in 1632. This school emphasized the importance of **mental discipline** and **strategy** in combat, producing many famous samurai, including Miyamoto Musashi. The samurai diet consisted mainly of **rice**, **miso soup**, and **vegetables**, with some samurai drinking up to 1 liter of **sake** per day. They also ate **umeboshi**, a type of pickled plum, to aid digestion. Some popular samurai dishes include:
1. **Sukiyaki**: a hot pot dish made with thinly sliced beef and vegetables.
2. **Shabu-shabu**: a hot pot dish made with thinly sliced meat and vegetables.
